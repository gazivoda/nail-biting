### Tool Profiles (5 × 200 words each = 1,000 words)

#### #1 — Habit Reversal Training (HRT)
**What it is:** Three-component CBT protocol (awareness training + competing response + social support). Gold standard with 70–90% reduction in clinical trials (Cochrane 2012). Self-guided via workbooks or therapist-led.

**Best for:** Anyone serious about stopping. Works for all nail biters, all severity levels.

**Limitations:** Requires consistent practice for 4–8 weeks. Hardest component — awareness — is difficult to achieve without external help.

**Pricing:** Free if self-guided (workbooks, online guides). $50–$150/session with a therapist specialising in BFRBs.

**Bottom line:** The evidence is clear — HRT is the most effective intervention for onychophagia. The challenge is the awareness gap: most biting happens automatically, below conscious threshold. If you can solve the awareness problem, HRT outcomes improve dramatically.

**Best for:** People who bite during screen-based work (coding, studying, browsing). The app watches passively while you work — no manual tracking required.

**Limitations:** Requires a webcam. Doesn't detect biting away from the computer. Designed for screen-time biting, not all-day monitoring.

**Pricing:** Free 3-day trial. €2.99/month or €29/year. No credit card required for trial.

#### #3 — Mavala Stop (Bitter Polish)
**What it is:** Colourless nail polish containing denatonium benzoate, the bitterest known substance. Applied to nails as a chemical deterrent. If your hand reaches your mouth, the taste is immediately unpleasant.

**Best for:** Early-stage or casual nail biters; people who bite without realising they're doing it; children (under adult supervision).

**Limitations:** Effectiveness drops sharply for established habits — habituated biters often adapt to the taste within 2–3 weeks. Requires consistent daily reapplication (washes off). Doesn't address the underlying trigger.

**Pricing:** ~€10–€12 per bottle. One bottle lasts 2–3 months with daily application.

**Evidence:** Moderate. Studies show deterrent effect in naïve biters, but limited data on long-term success in chronic onychophagia. Works best as an adjunct alongside HRT, not as a standalone solution.

#### #4 — Orly No Bite (Bitter Polish)
**What it is:** Direct competitor to Mavala Stop, same mechanism (denatonium benzoate deterrent), slightly different formulation. Long-wearing formula marketed as lasting 3–5 days between applications.

**Best for:** Same profile as Mavala Stop. Useful if Mavala wears off too quickly.

**Limitations:** Same fundamental limitation — habituation is faster than cessation in established biters.

**Pricing:** ~$9–$11 per bottle.

**Evidence:** Same tier as Mavala Stop. No head-to-head RCT with Mavala has been published. Choose based on availability and personal preference.

---

#### #5 — Competing Response / Fidget Tools
**What it is:** Physical objects that occupy the hands during high-risk contexts — fidget rings, textured objects, rubber bands on the wrist. These are the "competing response" component of HRT without the full protocol.

**Best for:** Supplementing other methods during identifiable trigger contexts (meetings, studying). Low-cost adjunct.

**Limitations:** Requires conscious implementation — doesn't fire automatically when the automatic habit triggers. Only helps in contexts where you proactively introduce the tool.

**Pricing:** $0–$20 depending on tool chosen.

**Evidence:** As a standalone method: limited. As part of HRT: essential. The competing response is the action that breaks the habit loop once awareness is achieved.

### How Each Works (400 words)

#### Mavala Stop: Chemical deterrent
Applied like clear nail polish to all fingernails. Denatonium benzoate (concentration: ~0.01%) produces an intensely bitter taste when nails enter the mouth. The discomfort creates a negative association intended to break the biting habit over 4–8 weeks.

Clinical mechanism: Classical conditioning (aversion therapy). The nail → mouth movement becomes paired with an unpleasant taste, theoretically weakening the habit through negative reinforcement.

**The habituation problem:** Most long-term nail biters report that the bitter taste becomes tolerable within 1–3 weeks. The brain adapts to predictable aversive stimuli, particularly when the underlying trigger (stress, boredom, focus) remains unaddressed. Studies on aversion therapy for BFRBs show high initial response rates but substantial relapse once the deterrent is removed or habituated to. Mavala's own instructions suggest 4–6 weeks of use — a timeline that presupposes the deterrent effect lasts.

**Where Mavala wins:** Simplicity and portability. No device, no subscription, works 24/7 including during sleep, eating, and away-from-screen contexts. For early-stage habits in casual biters, the deterrent alone is often sufficient.

#### Stop Biting: Real-time AI detection
Uses your existing webcam to run MediaPipe's hand-landmark and face-mesh models locally. When the AI detects your hand approaching your mouth with the characteristic posture of nail biting, it triggers an immediate audible alert — before the bite completes.

Clinical mechanism: Awareness training (the primary active ingredient in Habit Reversal Training). The alert fires at the precise moment the automatic habit begins, creating the conscious interruption that the habit loop requires. Over time, repeated interruptions weaken the automatic chain through non-reinforcement (operant extinction).

**The awareness advantage:** The core difficulty in stopping nail biting is that the behaviour is automatic — it happens below conscious awareness. Research shows most chronic biters notice fewer than half of their daily episodes. Stop Biting closes this gap mechanistically, not through willpower.

**Where Stop Biting is limited:** Requires a webcam and screen-based context. Doesn't monitor biting away from the computer.
